Disease focus · high unmet need oncology

Prioritising cancers where new metabolic strategies are most needed.

We focus on tumour types with persistently poor outcomes, where metabolic and mitochondrial adaptation can be a major driver of survival, therapy resistance, and immune evasion. Our goal is to translate mitochondria-centric insights into measurable, protocolised programs.

View pipeline Scientific platform

Selection criteria

  • High unmet need and limited durable responses
  • Clear mitochondrial/metabolic dysregulation signals
  • Feasible translational biomarkers for early readouts
  • Path to protocolised clinical evaluation

Why these cancers

Many solid tumours with poor survival demonstrate strong metabolic plasticity: they rewire energy production, redox balance, and nutrient utilisation to thrive under hypoxia, immune pressure, and therapy. Because mitochondria integrate these processes with apoptosis signalling and stress responses, a mitochondria-centric strategy may provide a differentiated way to identify vulnerabilities and design combinable interventions.

We are primarily interested in cancers with low five-year survival rates and high treatment resistance. These are areas where improved mechanistic understanding and disciplined, measurement-first development can have meaningful impact.

Pancreatic cancer

High metabolic adaptability and stromal constraints; need for new vulnerability-driven strategies.

Liver cancer

Complex metabolic environment and immune context; opportunities for biomarker-guided approaches.

Oesophageal cancer

Often diagnosed late with aggressive biology; rationale for targeting stress tolerance pathways.

Brain cancers

Therapy resistance and microenvironment constraints; require strong mechanistic and delivery-aware development.

Stomach (gastric) cancer

Heterogeneous disease with metabolic dependencies in subsets; opportunity for stratified programs.

Program expansion

As data mature, we will expand indications based on biomarker fit and translational feasibility.

What “layered metabolic strategy” means

We believe a layered approach to metabolic targeting can improve the probability of durable responses by addressing tumour survival from multiple angles. Practically, this means building programs that combine:

  • Cell-fate leverage — shifting apoptotic priming and stress signalling
  • Metabolic constraints — limiting escape routes such as glycolysis dominance or lactate coupling
  • Microenvironment compatibility — reducing factors that support immune suppression and invasion
  • Protocolised execution — predefined biomarkers, dosing logic, and milestone-driven escalation

How we translate into programs

Indications are selected based on a combination of biology, feasibility, and measurement. Our development approach is built to generate early, decision-quality evidence — not just mechanistic narratives.

Biomarker-first design

Define measurable readouts of mitochondrial/metabolic state early to guide selection and dosing.

Stratification hypotheses

Where possible, identify patient subsets most likely to benefit based on biology and biomarkers.

Combination readiness

Design programs to be combinable with standard-of-care and immune approaches as data support.

Responsible communication

We aim to be ambitious but rigorous. Programs are hypothesis-driven and milestone-gated, with claims anchored to measurable validation and a clear translational plan.
